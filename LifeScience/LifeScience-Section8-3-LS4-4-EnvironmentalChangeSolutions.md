## INVESTIGATION SESSIONS

### Session 1: Environmental Change Problems (60-75 minutes)

**Objective**: Identify environmental changes that affect organisms and analyze the problems they cause.

---

#### Part 1: How Environments Change (20 minutes)

**Discussion**:

**Adult Asks**:
- "Can environments change over time?"
  * Yes - we learned this in Section 6 (fossils showed ancient environments were different)
- "What can cause environments to change?"
  * Student brainstorms

**Two Types of Environmental Change**:

**1. Natural Changes** (slower, organisms can sometimes adapt):
- Climate shifts over thousands of years
- Natural disasters (volcano, earthquake, flood)
- Seasonal changes
- Natural fire cycles
- Aging of ecosystems

**2. Human-Caused Changes** (faster, harder for organisms to adapt):
- Cutting down forests (deforestation)
- Building cities/roads (habitat loss)
- Pollution (air, water, land)
- Climate change (burning fossil fuels)
- Overhunting/overfishing
- Introducing invasive species

**Key Difference**:
- Natural changes: Usually slow, organisms have time to adapt or move
- Human changes: Often fast, organisms can't adapt quickly enough

**Focus**: This section addresses human-caused changes we can help solve

**Student Creates Chart**:
```
Environmental Change | Cause | Effect on Organisms
---------------------|-------|---------------------
Forest cut down      | Humans clear land | Animals lose homes, food
Ocean pollution      | Plastic waste | Sea animals eat plastic, get hurt
Wetlands drained     | Building development | Water birds lose nesting sites
Climate warming      | Greenhouse gases | Ice melts, polar animals lose habitat
```

**Example - Ocean Plastic Pollution**:

**Problem**: Plastic waste ending up in oceans

**What's changing?**:
- Millions of tons of plastic enter ocean yearly
- Plastic doesn't decompose, breaks into small pieces
- Accumulates in ocean, beaches, and food chain

**Organisms affected**:
- Sea turtles: Eat plastic bags thinking they're jellyfish
- Seabirds: Feed plastic to chicks, can't digest, starve
- Fish: Eat microplastics, get stuck in plastic rings
- Whales: Swallow large plastic items, block digestive system
- Coral: Covered by plastic, can't get sunlight

**Impacts**:
- Animals die from eating plastic
- Get tangled in plastic (fishing nets, six-pack rings)
- Habitat polluted, unhealthy environment
- Population numbers decreasing

**Evidence**:
- Ocean Conservancy statistics
- Photos of affected animals
- Scientific studies showing plastic in fish stomachs

**Student Documents** (20 minutes):
- Research using approved websites, videos, books
- Take notes in template
- Collect images showing the problem
- Adult helps find age-appropriate sources

#### Part 3: Problem Impact Analysis (10-15 minutes)

**Activity**: Understand full scope of problem

**Student Answers**:

**Short-term effects** (now):
- What's happening to organisms right now?
- Are any dying or struggling?

**Long-term effects** (future):
- If problem continues, what will happen?
- Could species go extinct?
- How will ecosystem change?

**Urgency level**:
- How quickly must we act?
- Is this an emergency or slower problem?

**Student Creates Impact Statement**:

"The problem of [environmental change] is affecting [organisms] by [specific impacts]. If we don't act, [future consequences]. This is [urgent/moderate/slow] priority because [reasoning]."

Example:
"The problem of ocean plastic pollution is affecting sea turtles, seabirds, and fish by causing them to eat plastic and die. If we don't act, these populations will continue to decline and some species could become extinct. This is urgent priority because millions of animals are already dying and the problem is getting worse."

### Session 2: Designing Solutions (75-90 minutes)

**Objective**: Apply engineering design process to create and evaluate solutions to environmental problems.

---

#### Part 1: Engineering Design Process Review (15 minutes)

**Steps to Solve Problems**:

**Student Records**:

**1. Define the Problem**:
- What exactly needs to be solved?
- Who/what is affected?

**2. Identify Criteria and Constraints**:
- Criteria = What the solution must do (goals)
- Constraints = Limitations (cost, time, materials, etc.)

**3. Brainstorm Solutions**:
- Think of many possible ideas
- Don't judge yet - all ideas welcome

**4. Select Best Solution**:
- Compare ideas against criteria
- Choose most promising

**5. Create Design Plan**:
- Detail how solution will work
- What materials needed?

**6. Test (or Evaluate)**:
- Would it work?
- What are benefits and drawbacks?

**7. Improve**:
- How could it be better?

**Apply to Environmental Problem**:

**Define Problem** (from Session 1 research):
Example: "Sea turtles are dying because they eat plastic bags in the ocean"

**Criteria** (what solution must do):
- Reduce plastic in ocean
- Be affordable
- Not harm marine life
- Actually work

**Constraints** (limitations):
- Can't remove ALL plastic instantly
- Limited money available
- Must be safe for environment
- Need people to cooperate

---

#### Part 2: Brainstorming Solutions (25-30 minutes)

**Activity**: Generate multiple solution ideas

**Brainstorming Rules**:
- Think of at least 5 different solutions
- No idea is too silly or simple
- Build on each other's ideas
- Don't judge or criticize yet

**Student Brainstorms** (for their chosen environmental problem):

**Example Solutions for Ocean Plastic**:

1. **Stop plastic at the source**:
   - Ban single-use plastic bags
   - Use reusable bags instead
   - Reduce plastic packaging

2. **Clean up existing plastic**:
   - Ocean cleanup boats with nets
   - Beach cleanup events
   - Robots to collect plastic

3. **Create better alternatives**:
   - Biodegradable bags (break down naturally)
   - Edible packaging
   - Paper or cloth instead of plastic

4. **Educate people**:
   - Teach about problem in schools
   - Advertising campaigns
   - Make it easy to recycle

5. **Technology solutions**:
   - Machines that eat plastic and convert to energy
   - Bacteria engineered to break down plastic
   - Drones to spot and collect floating plastic

**For Each Idea, Student Notes**:
- How it would work
- What it would cost (cheap/moderate/expensive)
- How effective it might be
- Any problems with the idea

**Create Idea Cards** (15 minutes):
- Index card for each solution
- Drawing or diagram
- Brief description
- Pros and cons list

---

#### Part 3: Evaluating and Selecting Solution (20-25 minutes)

**Activity**: Compare solutions and choose best one(s)

**Create Evaluation Matrix**:

```
Solution | Helps Organisms? | Affordable? | Easy to Do? | Effective? | TOTAL
---------|------------------|-------------|-------------|------------|-------
Idea 1   | ___/5           | ___/5       | ___/5       | ___/5      | ___/20
Idea 2   | ___/5           | ___/5       | ___/5       | ___/5      | ___/20
[etc.]
```

**Rating Scale**: 1 = poor, 3 = okay, 5 = excellent

**Student Rates Each Solution**:
- Be honest about pros and cons
- Consider multiple factors
- Add up total scores

**Discussion** (Adult guides):

**Adult Asks**:
- "Which solution scored highest?"
- "Does highest score always mean best?"
  * Not always - some factors more important than others
- "What if a solution helps a lot but is very expensive?"
  * Trade-offs exist
- "Can we combine solutions?"
  * Yes! Often multiple approaches work together

**Student Selects** (can choose 1-2 solutions):
- Top-scoring solution, OR
- Combination of solutions, OR
- Solution with best balance of factors

**Justify Choice**:
"I chose [solution] because ___. Even though it has the limitation of ___, the benefits of ___ make it the best option."

## Troubleshooting

**"The problem seems too big to solve"**:
- Focus on one part of the problem
- Small solutions can add up
- We can all contribute something
- Don't need to solve everything at once

**"My solution wouldn't really work"**:
- This is a learning exercise
- Real-world solutions take teams of experts
- Your thinking process is what matters
- Acknowledge limitations honestly

**"I can't choose between solutions"**:
- That's realistic! Trade-offs are real
- Can propose combination of solutions
- Explain why choice is difficult
- Present top 2-3 with evaluation

**"This makes me sad/worried"**:
- Environmental problems are serious
- But people ARE working on solutions
- Student can make difference through choices
- Focus on hope and action, not just problems
